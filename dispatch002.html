The Owners

A journey through the countries that learned to own the future—and those that did not.

In 2064, the great argument is no longer about who has a job. It is about who owns the machines, the intelligence and the infrastructure that make jobs less necessary—and who still gets to decide what happens next. Across the world, governments have begun giving citizens a share of the future: dividends from automated companies, stakes in public funds, rights to data, energy and housing. But as ownership spreads, a harder question remains. Receiving a share of the wealth is not always the same as having the power to shape it.

Approx. 40 minute read

Part I — The Last Paycheck

The Girl on Platform Seven

The girl wanted Patagonia. Her mother wanted compound interest. They had been arguing for forty-three minutes by the time the train reached Lausanne.

"I'll put it back."

"You don't know that."

"I do."

"You said that about Iceland."

"I did put it back."

"After three years."

The girl sighed dramatically enough for half the carriage to hear.

"I'm seventeen."

"Exactly."

"I don't want to climb Patagonia when I'm forty."

"You won't. You'll thank yourself when you're forty."

"I'd rather remember Patagonia."

The mother looked out of the window, as if Switzerland might settle the argument for her. Rain followed the lake. The mountains disappeared into low clouds. Neither of them noticed.

"Three percent," the girl said.

"It's still three percent."

"Three percent becomes five."

"Five becomes eight."

"Eight becomes..."

"I know how compound interest works."

"No," her mother replied quietly.

"You know how the calculator works."

Silence. The girl folded her arms.

"I hate talking about money."

Her mother smiled.

"This isn't money."

It was one of those family conversations that felt too intimate to overhear and too unusual to ignore. I was sitting across the aisle with a notebook on my lap, pretending to read briefing papers for a conference in Geneva. I had been covering economics for nearly twenty years. I had interviewed finance ministers, central bankers, founders, union leaders and more billionaires than I care to admit. I understood inflation. Interest rates. Public debt. Trade wars. Artificial intelligence. At least I thought I did.I had absolutely no idea what these two were talking about.

The conductor announced the next station. The girl reached into her bag and pulled out a transparent screen. She touched it twice. A chart appeared. It looked remarkably like an investment portfolio.

"Look."

She turned the screen towards her mother .

"If I sell three percent now..."

"I know."

"...I'll still have more than enough."

"I know."

"I've done the maths."

"I know."

The mother closed the screen.

"You've only calculated numbers."

"What else is there?"

For the first time, the mother hesitated. Then she asked a question that seemed strangely difficult.

"What happens if one day you need permission?"

The girl frowned.

"Permission from who?"

"Exactly."

The conversation ended there. Not because they agreed. Because the train had arrived.

The girl kissed her mother on the cheek. Shouldered a climbing backpack that looked larger than she was. And disappeared into the station.

I watched her go.

Then I wrote one sentence in my notebook. Find out what she meant by permission.

At the time, I thought that would be the story. I was wrong.

Geneva

There are conferences where history announces itself. This was not one of them. History had apparently arrived yesterday. Everything about the Global Forum on Productive Participation suggested careful moderation. Grey carpets. White coffee cups. Name badges printed in the same typeface governments had been using since the early twenty-first century. Even the title had clearly survived several committees.

Nobody called it that. The taxi driver called it The Ownership Circus. The newspaper kiosks called it Geneva Week. The protesters outside preferred The Auction. Only diplomats insisted on the official name.

Three demonstrations occupied the square outside the Palais des Nations. The first demanded universal ownership of advanced intelligence. The second demanded that advanced intelligence could never be owned. The third demanded something much older .

BRING BACK REAL JOBS

They were the loudest. They were also the smallest.

As I crossed the square, I noticed something odd. People were arguing passionately. But not about work. Not really. They argued about rights. Voting rights. Data rights. Inheritance rights. Training rights. Licensing rights. Governance rights.

Nobody seemed particularly interested in employment. It was as if everyone had quietly accepted that chapter had already been written.

Inside, the auditorium filled slowly. Three thousand delegates. Economists. Engineers. Teachers. Mayors. Trade union leaders. People wearing expensive watches. People wearing sandals. A surprising number of teenagers.

That puzzled me.

Economic conferences normally contain only people who enjoy economic conferences. Teenagers have better things to do.

The woman opening the session walked on stage carrying nothing except a tablet. No fanfare. No dramatic music. No inspirational video.

"Good morning."

The room answered in dozens of accents.

She smiled.

"Before we begin..."

She paused just long enough for conversations to stop.

"...I'd like to ask a very simple question."

The first slide appeared. No graphs. No logos.Just one sentence.

How many of you expect wages to become your largest source of income again?

Please raise your hand.

Silence.

People looked around. A few hands went up. Perhaps fifty. No more.

The woman nodded politely.

"Thank you."

She touched the screen again. The next slide.

How many of you hope they don't?

Almost everyone raised a hand. Nobody laughed. Nobody seemed surprised.

The moderator thanked them and continued with her presentation.

I stopped listening. Not because it wasn't interesting. Because something had just happened that nobody else appeared to find remarkable. I looked around the room. Three thousand intelligent people. Not one had reacted.

The thought arrived slowly. History is difficult to notice when everyone else has already adjusted to it.

During the coffee break I cornered a Finnish minister whose interviews had always been pleasantly disappointing. He answered questions directly. Journalists dislike this because it leaves us with less work to do.

"Minister ."

He smiled.

"We've met."

"I know."

"You've forgotten my name."

"I've forgotten everyone's name."

I pointed towards the auditorium.

"Can I ask you something?"

"What just happened?"

He looked over my shoulder .

"The opening session."

"No."

"The question."

"No."

He stirred his coffee.

"You'll have to help me."

"Nobody raised their hand."

"So?"

"So..."

I realised I couldn't finish the sentence. He watched me with patient curiosity.

"When did wages stop being normal?"

He smiled.

"Wrong question."

"What should I ask instead?"

He took a sip.

"When did we stop expecting wages to do everything?"

Before I could respond, someone interrupted to congratulate him on a report I hadn't read. He excused himself. As he walked away, he turned back.

"Go to Poland."

"Why?"

"They still remember the transition."

Then he disappeared into the crowd.

Adam

My train left Geneva just after six. European trains had become quieter over the years. Not because the engines improved. Because conversations had changed.

People spoke less into phones.More into earpieces invisible to everyone else. Entire negotiations now looked like thoughtful silence.

The woman opposite spent forty minutes staring out of the window. Twice she nodded. Once she frowned.

At Basel she shook someone's hand. Nobody had entered the carriage. I missed ordinary phone calls.

Warsaw greeted me with drizzle. Adam insisted on meeting in a milk bar.

"People always want to eat in fashionable places," he wrote.

"They learn nothing."

The milk bar occupied the ground floor of a building that had survived three political systems and at least four interior redesigns. Nothing inside suggested optimism. The fluorescent lights buzzed faintly. The menu had been laminated enough times to qualify as archaeology.

An elderly woman behind the counter looked at me with the expression of someone who had correctly predicted my order before I walked in.

Adam waved from the corner. He stood to shake my hand. Sixty-eight. Grey hair. Green jacket. The sort of face that permanently appears five minutes away from making a sarcastic observation.

"You've lost weight."

"So have you."

"I planned it."

"So did I."

He laughed.

"Liar ."

We ordered. I asked for coffee. Adam looked horrified.

"You came to Poland for coffee?"

"I came to interview you."

"Exactly."

He turned towards the counter.

"Two żurek."

When the waitress left, he folded his hands on the table.

"So."

"So."

"You're here because of Geneva."

"How do you know?"

"You've got the conference bag."

I looked down. The logo was still hanging from my shoulder .

"Damn."

"Journalists always forget the obvious things."

I took out my notebook.

"I've got a question."

"I know."

"What happened?"

He laughed so loudly that two pensioners looked over from the next table.

"There."

"What?"

"That's exactly why Finland sent you."

"What do you mean?"

"You still think something happened."

He leaned back.

"No."

"What?"

"The future."

"It didn't arrive."

"It accumulated."

He picked up the spoon.

"Tell me."

"Do you remember COVID?"

"Of course."

"What changed?"

"Remote work."

"Good."

"What else?"

"Supply chains."

"Good."

"What else?"

"AI accelerated."

"Good."

"What else?"

I searched my memory. People wearing masks. Empty streets. Video meetings. Restaurants closing. Governments printing unimaginable amounts of money. Silence.

"I don't know."

Adam nodded.

"Exactly."

"You don't know."

The soup arrived.

He waited until the waitress walked away.

"Everyone remembers the virus."

"Yes."

"Almost nobody remembers what it taught."

"What did it teach?"

He broke the bread slowly.

"That society could survive enormous changes."

"I don't follow."

"You will."

He dipped the bread into the soup.

"COVID taught governments that impossible things were often just expensive things."

I wrote that down.

He noticed.

"Don't write that yet."

"Why not?"

"Because that's still not the story."

"Then what is?"

Adam smiled.

"The story..."

He pointed his spoon towards the window where people hurried through the rain.

"...begins with a generation that stopped believing salaries could buy the lives their parents had promised them."

Outside, a delivery robot rolled carefully across the pedestrian crossing. Neither of us watched it. At least, not yet.

Part II — The Generation That Lost Faith

Adam refused to talk about economics until we had finished the soup. This, he explained, was one of the advantages of getting older .

"When you're young," he said, "you think every conversation has to arrive somewhere."

"And when you're old?"

"You realise most conversations are simply trying to figure out what the question is."

He tore another piece of bread.

"You came because something strange happened in Geneva."

"You think salaries disappeared."

"I think they became... less important."

"Good."

"That's progress."

"From what?"

"Yesterday you thought they disappeared."

I waited.

Adam enjoyed waiting. Some people use silence to think. Adam used it to make other people think.

"You know what the biggest mistake historians make?" he asked.

"They assume people notice history while it's happening."

"They don't?"

He shook his head.

"History is almost always mistaken for bad news."

He asked me to remember 2026. Not the headlines. The ordinary Tuesday. The man scrolling job adverts while sitting on the toilet.The university graduate calculating how many years of saving would be needed for a mortgage. The delivery rider checking three different apps because one paid twenty cents more per order. The software engineer wondering whether the new AI assistant was making him twice as productive or quietly rehearsing his replacement. The couple postponing children.

Again.

The accountant who had started a YouTube channel "just in case." The teacher selling lesson plans online. The architect experimenting with image generators after midnight. The nurse buying exchange-traded funds every payday because everyone on the internet said long-term investing was the only sensible thing left.

"Remember them?"

"I was one of them."

"So was I."

He smiled.

"That's why people misunderstand that period."

"How?"

"They think everyone suddenly became obsessed with investing."

"They became obsessed with escape."

The waitress returned to refill our tea.

Adam waited until she left.

"Do you know why prediction markets became popular?"

"Because people liked gambling."

"Partly."

"They were one of the few places where a twenty-year-old could imagine changing their life."

He pointed at me with his spoon.

"You journalists called them reckless."

"Weren't they?"

"Some were."

"And the others?"

"They had simply stopped believing."

"In what?"

He didn't answer immediately. He looked out of the rain-streaked window.

"In linear lives."

"What do you mean by a linear life?"

"The old promise."

He counted on his fingers.

"Study."

"Job."

"Promotion."

"House."

"Pension."

"Retirement."

He shrugged.

"It had worked well enough for three generations."

"And then?"

"Then mathematics happened."

He took a napkin and drew two lines. One climbed gently. The other rose almost vertically.

"Wages."

He pointed at the first.

"Assets."

He pointed at the second.

"This is simplified."

"Obviously."

"But emotionally..."

He tapped the table.

"...this is what millions of people believed."

"Was it true?"

He smiled.

"Does it matter?"

"If enough people believe they're falling behind..."

"What did they do?"

"They looked for shortcuts."

The strange thing, Adam explained, was that almost every shortcut looked ridiculous. People mocked cryptocurrency. Then central banks created digital currencies. People mocked influencers.

Then companies spent more on creators than television. People mocked teenagers selling digital products.

Then universities taught courses on building online businesses. People mocked prediction markets. Then governments quietly began using them to forecast elections, epidemics and energy demand.

"History," Adam said, "has an irritating habit."

"It steals ideas from clowns."

Adam smiled.

"You still want one cause."

"There wasn't one?"

"There never is."

He asked me another question.

"Do you remember how people talked during COVID?"

"They talked about resilience."

"They talked about supply chains."

"They talked about remote work."

"They talked about essential workers."

He nodded.

"What else?"

I thought for a while.

"I don't know."

"They discovered governments could rewrite economic rules in a weekend."

He let the sentence hang.

"Think about it."

"Countries closed economies."

"They paid millions of people not to work."

"They guaranteed loans."

"They froze evictions."

"They invented programmes nobody believed possible six months earlier ."

I nodded slowly.

"So?"

"So..."

He leaned forward.

"...people stopped believing the economy was a law of nature."

He paid the bill before I could object.

Outside, the rain had stopped. Warsaw smelled of wet pavement.

We walked towards Plac Konstytucji. The city had changed enormously since my first visit decades earlier.

Glass towers reflected socialist apartment blocks. Autonomous trams glided almost silently. Students filled cafés that had once been banks. A delivery robot rolled between pedestrians carrying groceries.

Nobody looked at it. Technology becomes invisible astonishingly quickly.

The Debate Nobody Wanted

The next morning Adam took me somewhere I hadn't expected. The National Museum.

"Why are we here?"

"Because every revolution eventually ends up in a museum."

"This one already has an exhibition?"

The exhibition was called:

The Ownership Debates: 2034–2042

Schoolchildren wandered between interactive displays. An elderly guide corrected a teenager who had confused an early Citizen Capital Account with a sovereign wealth fund. The teenager apologised. Apparently everyone learned this in school now.

The first display wasn't about housing. A wall-sized screen showed apartment prices from dozens of countries between 1995 and 2035. The lines climbed relentlessly. Beside them, wages. Almost flat. No commentary. None was needed.

The next room displayed screenshots from social media. Posts from the late 2020s. Young people joking that retirement meant "dying at work." Memes about never owning a house. Videos celebrating twenty-year-olds who had become millionaires trading obscure digital assets.

Next to them, another screen. Suicide statistics. Fertility rates. Average age of first-time home ownership. The humour looked different beside the numbers.

Adam watched me reading.

"They weren't irrational."

"They were desperate."

"Sometimes those look identical."

The final room recreated a parliamentary debate. Actors. Original recordings. Archived interviews. Visitors could sit in the old parliamentary benches while holographic politicians argued around them. A conservative minister spoke first.

"If labour no longer captures the value created by our economy..."

He paused.

"...then ownership must become broader."

Boos. Applause. A socialist leader stood.

"This proposal does not reform capitalism."

"It rescues it."

More applause. More boos.

A banking representative warned that governments should not become investment managers. Trade unions demanded stronger wages instead. Young representatives asked a different question.

"If wages no longer buy a future..."

They looked around the chamber .

"...what exactly are you asking us to defend?"

The recording ended. No winner. No triumphant music. Only uncertainty.

"Who won?" I asked.

Adam smiled.

"Nobody."

"Then why did Citizen Capital Accounts happen?"

"Because everyone lost."

I looked at him.

"The housing crisis."

"The demographic crisis."

"The pension crisis."

"The AI productivity shock."

"The political paralysis."

He counted them one by one.

"No single crisis was big enough."

"Together ..."

"They cornered everyone."

That afternoon I sat alone in my hotel room reading parliamentary archives. One sentence kept appearing. Different parties. Different countries. Different years. The wording changed. The idea didn't.

If citizens cannot realistically accumulate productive assets through wages alone, the state must decide whether ownership remains a privilege or becomes infrastructure.

I read it again. Ownership...as infrastructure.

I suddenly remembered the girl on the train.

"This isn't money."

Her mother hadn't been talking about money. She had been talking about citizenship.

I closed the laptop.

For the first time since Geneva, I felt as though I was beginning to understand the question. Which, in retrospect, was exactly the moment I should have become suspicious. Because every time I thought I understood this story...someone showed me another country.

Adam called that evening.

"Tomorrow morning."

"What?"

"Stockholm."

"Why?"

"They solved one problem."

"They accidentally created another."

Part III — The Country That Solved It

If Warsaw still remembered the transition, Stockholm behaved as though there had never been one.

The first thing I noticed was the absence of hurry. People moved with purpose, but not urgency. At half past ten on a Wednesday morning, cafés were full. Cyclists stopped to chat. Parents collected children from school in the middle of the day. Nobody looked guilty about not being at work. The city had become rich enough to stop pretending busyness was a virtue. Or so I thought.

Elin met me outside a bakery overlooking the water. She was thirty-one. She apologised for being late.

"I had a board meeting."

"What company?"

She laughed.

"I have no idea."

I assumed she was joking.

She wasn't.

She explained that one of her citizen funds rotated ordinary members through governance panels. Four times a year she spent three hours voting on broad investment mandates for a portfolio she could barely describe.

"Do you enjoy it?"

"No."

"Then why do it?"

"They pay me."

"How much?"

She shrugged.

"Enough for lunch."

"And the decisions?"

"Do you understand them?"

She looked embarrassed.

"Not really."

We walked toward her apartment. On the way we passed a playground. Children were arguing. Not about football. Not about who cheated. About a desalination project.

"I think the Mediterranean allocation is too high."

"No, because southern migration projections changed."

"You didn't read the report."

"My dad did."

I looked at Elin.

"They're ten."

"They're competitive."

"No..."

"They're discussing infrastructure."

She smiled.

"They have a civics simulation."

"When I was ten we played Monopoly."

"So did we."

"What do children play now?"

She thought about it.

"Mostly cooperative games."

"Why?"

"Nobody likes Monopoly anymore."

Her apartment overlooked the harbour. Not large. Not luxurious. Just comfortable. Books. Plants. An old guitar. A coffee machine that had probably survived three governments.

"You live here alone?"

"Yes."

"You own it?"

"No."

The answer surprised me.

"You don't?"

"I own part of the building."

"I don't understand."

"Neither does my grandmother."

She laughed.

"She still says renting."

Stockholm had quietly rewritten housing. Entire apartment blocks belonged to resident trusts. People accumulated equity without purchasing entire homes. Prices still rose. Speculation still existed. But no one needed to buy an entire building to gain security.

It was one of dozens of reforms that sounded terribly boring until someone explained what life had been like before.

Over lunch I asked the question that had been following me since Geneva.

"What do you do?"

She hesitated.

"I don't know what you mean."

"For work."

"Oh."

She smiled apologetically.

"I design medical training environments."

"Full time?"

"No."

"Three days?"

"Usually two."

"And the rest?"

"The rest what?"

"The rest of your income."

She began counting.

"My work."

"My capital account."

"My cooperative."

"A small royalty."

"A municipal energy dividend."

She stopped.

"I think that's everything."

"You think?"

"Things change."

She took another sip of coffee.

"My father still asks me what my job is."

"And?"

"I tell him."

"No."

"You tell him what?"

"What I build."

That afternoon she took me to the school where her niece studied. The classroom looked ordinary. Maps. Books. Children.

One wall displayed family trees. Another displayed something called Household Income Ecology. Each child had drawn where money entered their family. Work. Dividends. Royalties. Businesses. Licensing. Pensions.

One drawing contained six streams flowing into the same river. The teacher noticed me staring.

"We stopped teaching careers."

"You what?"

"We still teach professions."

"Then what changed?"

"We teach resilience differently."

She walked toward the wall.

"For a century resilience meant having one stable job."

She pointed at the drawings.

"Now it means depending on several fragile things."

I wrote that sentence down. Then I crossed it out. It sounded clever. Too clever. Real life is rarely that tidy.

On my last evening in Stockholm I asked Elin the question Adam had taught me.

"Who owns the delivery robots?"

She looked at me strangely.

"Everyone."

"No."

"I mean..."

"Who decides where they go?"

She stopped walking.

"I've honestly never thought about it."

"You receive income from them."

"I suppose."

"But who gives them instructions?"

She frowned.

"The company."

"Who controls the company?"

"The board."

"Who appoints the board?"

"The fund."

"Who controls the fund?"

She laughed.

"I see what you're doing."

"What?"

"You're turning ownership into power ."

"Isn't it?"

She looked across the water for a long time.

"I don't know."

It was the first uncertain answer I had heard in Stockholm.

California

Noah and Daniel had graduated from the same university. Shared the same apartment. Worked at the same company. Lost the same jobs.

By every measure, history should have treated them similarly. History rarely cares about symmetry.

Noah met me in a quiet café. There were no employees. Coffee emerged from a machine that apologised if extraction exceeded twenty-eight seconds.

"What do you own?" I asked.

He laughed.

"You've been to Europe."

"How can you tell?"

"You didn't ask what I do."

He supervised seven autonomous companies. None employed people. Each employed contracts. Models. Software. Other companies.

His weekly workload fluctuated between twelve and twenty hours.

"So you're retired?"

"No."

"So you're working?"

"No."

"What then?"

"I'm responsible."

Daniel arrived twenty minutes later. They hugged awkwardly. Old friends often do.

Over dinner I asked them the same question.

"What happened?"

Noah answered first.

"We built."

Daniel interrupted.

"We inherited."

Silence.

"What do you mean?"

Daniel pointed at Noah.

"His parents had a house."

Noah nodded.

"I did."

"I didn't."

Another silence.

"When our company collapsed..."

Daniel continued.

"...I used my severance to pay rent."

"Noah?"

"He bought compute."

The room became very quiet. One family had possessed a spare bedroom. The other hadn't.

History entered through the front door disguised as real estate.

After dinner Daniel walked me back toward my hotel.

"You've been asking everyone what they own."

"I have."

"Wrong question."

"So what's the right one?"

He stopped beneath a streetlight.

"What can you lose tomorrow without asking anyone?"

I didn't answer .

He continued.

"I own ETFs."

"Pensions."

"Citizen funds."

"I own tiny pieces of hundreds of companies."

"But if I stop working..."

He shrugged.

"...everything else becomes a countdown."

"You've got almost two hundred thousand dollars in assets."

"I know."

"That's not enough?"

"It buys security."

"Not independence."

The distinction hit me harder than I expected. Security. Independence. Ownership. Power.

The words had spent forty years pretending to be synonyms.They weren't.

The Man Who Refused to Buy Coffee

The philosopher wasn't a philosopher. At least not professionally. His name was Emil Sørensen. He had spent thirty years designing public spaces before universities began inviting him to explain why cities no longer behaved the way economists expected.

We met in Copenhagen.

He refused to buy coffee.

"The café is beautiful."

"It is."

"So why aren't we going inside?"

"Because every conversation eventually becomes a transaction."

"And?"

"I'd like ours not to."

We sat on a bench overlooking the harbour. I explained my journey. Warsaw. Stockholm. California. Citizen capital. Ownership. Freedom. He listened without interruption.

When I finished, he asked one question.

"Have you noticed what everyone is trying to save?"

I thought for a moment.

"The economy?"

"No."

"Capitalism?"

"No."

"Democracy?"

He smiled.

"They're all trying to save the Industrial Age."

"I don't follow."

"For two hundred years we organised society around one assumption."

He drew a circle in the air .

"Adults sell labour ."

"They receive wages."

"They buy life."

He looked at me.

"Every institution grew around that."

"Schools."

"Pensions."

"Mortgages."

"Taxes."

"Identity."

I nodded.

"And now?"

"Now labour matters less."

"So we replaced wages with assets."

"Yes."

He shook his head gently.

"That's an answer ."

"You disagree?"

"I think it's an answer to yesterday's question."

"What should today's question be?"

He looked across the harbour .

"What must never become a market?"

The sentence hung between us.

He continued.

"Every civilisation answers that differently."

"Some say votes."

"Some say children."

"Some say organs."

"Some say rivers."

He turned back toward me.

"You've spent three weeks asking who should own the future."

"Yes."

"I think you're asking too little."

"What should I ask?"

"What parts of the future should nobody own?"

I didn't know how to answer. Neither, he admitted, did he.

Geneva, Again

The conference was ending when I returned. Delegates hurried through the corridors carrying tote bags filled with reports that would almost certainly remain unread.

The final panel was titled:

Ownership, Citizenship and Human Flourishing.

I almost skipped it. Instead I stood at the back. Questions came from the audience.

A woman from Brazil asked about data rights. A Kenyan cooperative argued for community ownership. A Japanese mayor wanted cities to own artificial intelligence directly.

Then someone near the front stood. I couldn't see his face.

"One question."

The room quietened.

"If everyone owns something..."

He paused.

"...who still has the power to say no?"

No one answered immediately. Not the ministers. Not the investors. Not the philosophers.

For the first time all week, silence won.

On the train to the airport I recognised Platform Seven. The same platform where I had first seen the girl arguing with her mother.

This time she was alone. Older by only a few weeks.

She recognised me before I recognised her.

"The journalist."

"The climber ."

She smiled.

"I went."

"To Patagonia?"

"I sold three percent."

"Your mother forgave you?"

"Eventually."

I hesitated.

"Can I ask you something?"

She nodded.

"Your mother said one day you might need permission."

"Yes."

"What did she mean?"

The girl looked down the platform where trains disappeared toward the mountains.

"When I was little," she said, "I thought being rich meant buying whatever you wanted."

"And now?"

"Now I think it means never having to ask."

"My mother wasn't talking about money."

"What was she talking about?"

She shouldered her backpack.

"She was talking about freedom."

Her train arrived.

The doors opened.

Before boarding, she turned back.

"Oh..."

"One more thing."

"Yes?"

"You've been asking the wrong people."

"Who should I ask?"

She smiled.

"The people who still live on their last paycheck."

Then she was gone.

I watched the train disappear into the distance. Across the tracks, a delivery robot rolled silently along the platform.

For weeks I had been asking who owned it. Standing there, I realised the question was incomplete. Ownership explained who received the income. It did not explain who wrote the destination.

Somewhere, someone had decided where that machine would go. Perhaps they owned it. Perhaps they merely managed it. Perhaps they had been elected. Perhaps nobody even knew their name.

I took out my notebook. On the first page, beneath a sentence I had written weeks earlier, I added one final line.

The first sentence still read:

Find out what she meant by permission.

Underneath it I wrote:

The next story begins with whoever gives it.
