Womb, Egg or Pouch?

I Spent Three Weeks With the Parents of 2055. Nobody Knows What They’re Doing.

The fertility crisis was supposed to be solved by giving humans three ways to reproduce. Instead, we got subscription incubators, pouch custody disputes and a new generation of parents nostalgic for the days when pregnancy was merely terrifying.

Approx. 20 minute read

At 7:42 on a wet Tuesday morning in Barcelona, an unborn child is asking for a software update. I am standing barefoot in the kitchen of Laura Méndez and Marc Vidal, staring at their daughter .

She is an egg. The egg is approximately the size of a small watermelon and sits inside a white glass chamber between a coffee machine and a bowl of oranges. A soft amber light moves across the shell. Somewhere inside, seven months into development, is a human being who currently weighs 2.6 kilograms and has never experienced weather .

The incubator makes a sound. Not an alarm exactly. More like the noise an expensive car makes when it wants you to know that something, somewhere, might require attention. Marc looks at his phone.

“Again?”

“What?” Laura asks.

“Humidity.”

The display on the incubator changes.

HUMIDITY VARIATION DETECTED

EMBRYONIC HEALTH: 99.4%

RECOMMENDED ACTION AVAILABLE

Marc taps the screen. A second message appears.

Upgrade to NestLife Premium Care to unlock detailed recommendations.

Nobody says anything. Rain hits the balcony. The coffee machine begins grinding beans. The unborn child continues to exist at 99.4%.

“Yesterday she was 99.6,” Marc says.

“Yesterday morning?”

“I think.”

“You think?”

“I have screenshots.”

He does. Marc has 318 screenshots of his unborn daughter’s health metrics. Laura looks at me.

“This,” she says, “is why I wanted a womb birth.”

Marc looks up from his phone.

“No, you didn’t.”

“I considered it.”

“You considered it for eleven minutes.”

“It was a serious eleven minutes.”

The incubator makes the sound again. Marc swears. This is my first morning with an Egg Family. By the end of the week, I will have held a portable incubation battery worth more than my first car , visited a government egg nursery beneath a shopping centre and watched two adults argue over whether their unborn child’s shell looked “slightly less matte than yesterday.”

After that, I will spend four days with a Pouch Family, where a six-month-old baby moves between the bodies of her parents according to a shared calendar . Finally, I will live with a woman doing something that, twenty years ago, would have been completely unremarkable.

She is nine months pregnant. In 2055, this makes her a radical.

The Kitchen Incubator

The first thing you notice about a human egg is that it has presence. A pregnancy can enter a room invisibly. An egg is furniture. Laura and Marc live in a two-bedroom apartment in Poblenou. They have a sofa, a dining table, two bicycles mounted on a wall and, in the centre of the kitchen, €11,000 worth of reproductive infrastructure.

There is the main incubator . There is the backup incubator . There is the emergency power unit. There is a portable travel cradle. There are three sealed humidity cartridges. There is a medical shell scanner .

There is a small red box that Marc tells me I must never touch.

“What’s in it?”

“Emergency stabilization foam.”

“What does it do?”

“We don’t know.”

“Have you read the instructions?”

“Yes.”

“And?”

“We still don’t know.”

Their daughter’s name is Alma. She has been outside Laura’s body for 183 days. Laura carried her for eight weeks. The egg was then extracted at a clinic and placed in a medical incubator . At twelve weeks, Laura and Marc brought it home.

I ask what that day felt like.

“Terrifying,” Laura says.

“Beautiful,” Marc says.

“Terrifying.”

“Beautiful and terrifying.”

“No. Mainly terrifying.”

The hospital gave them a two-hour training course. This covered temperature management, emergency transport, power failures and something called

“unexpected shell activity.”

“What is unexpected shell activity?” I ask.

“We asked that,” Laura says.

“What did they say?”

“That we would know.”

I look at the egg. The egg does nothing. Laura pours coffee. Like many people of their generation, she chose external incubation partly because she did not want pregnancy to derail her work. She is an architect. Marc works in cybersecurity. Both are 36.

They had been discussing children for six years. The conversations always ended the same way. Not now. After the next promotion. After the apartment. After interest rates come down.

After the project. After summer . After Christmas. After life becomes more stable. Life did not become more stable. Then Spain expanded the Free Egg Program. Every citizen became entitled to one basic incubator , emergency power support and access to municipal incubation centres.

Laura and Marc signed up.

“Did the program make you decide to have a child?” I ask.

Laura thinks about this.

“It removed some excuses.”

Marc looks offended.

“They weren’t excuses.”

“They were very sophisticated excuses.”

The government incubator was free. They returned it after three weeks.

“It was completely safe,” Marc tells me.

“What was wrong with it?”

“The app was terrible.”

They bought the NestLife Pro. The base model costs €3,800. Then came the accessories. Then the subscriptions. Then the backup systems. Then the insurance. I ask how much they have spent.

Marc says €8,000. Laura says €11,000. Marc says some of those purchases were unnecessary. Laura asks which ones. Marc changes the subject. The egg sits between us. I begin to understand something about the Egg Economy.

The technology did not remove parental anxiety. It gave anxiety a product catalogue.

The Egg District

On Wednesday, I travel with Laura and Alma to a municipal incubation centre. Transporting an egg requires preparation. First, Laura disconnects the primary monitoring cable. Then she attaches a portable sensor .

Then she places the egg inside a shock-absorbing travel shell. Then the shell goes inside a temperature-controlled carrier . Then the carrier connects to a battery. The entire procedure takes nine minutes.

I ask whether it would be easier to leave the egg at home.

“Yes,” Laura says.

“Then why are we bringing her?”

Laura looks at me as though I have suggested leaving a toddler in a cupboard.

“She has an appointment.”

We take the metro. There are designated egg spaces near the doors. They look like luggage racks designed by a neonatal intensive care unit. A young man is already using one. His egg is in a transparent carrier covered with stickers.

One says:

BABY ON BOARD

Another says:

DO NOT TAP THE SHELL

A third says:

GRANDMA LOADING…

Laura notices me looking.

“First egg,” she whispers.

“How can you tell?”

“Stickers.”

At Diagonal station, an elderly woman gets on and immediately starts asking about the eggs.

How old?

How long until hatching?

Natural shell or reinforced?

The young man answers politely. Laura looks at her phone. Alma: 99.7%. She smiles.

“What happened?”

“Nothing.”

“Is that good?”

“Very.”

Five minutes earlier , 99.4% was apparently a crisis. Now 99.7% is joy. The municipal incubation centre is located beneath a shopping complex. Above us, people buy trainers. Below us, 214 unborn humans develop in climate-controlled cabinets.

The room is enormous and very quiet. Rows of incubators stretch behind glass. Some eggs are white. Some are cream. Some are pale grey. One has a football club scarf draped around the base.

A technician named Óscar meets us. He has worked here for six years.

“Do parents leave them overnight?” I ask.

“Of course.”

“For how long?”

“Some people leave them for months.”

“Months?”

Óscar shrugs.

“People travel.”

He leads me past the long-term incubation wing. Every egg has a small screen. Names. Development stage. Medical status. Contact information. One screen displays a photograph of two parents standing on a beach in Thailand.

Their unborn child is in Barcelona.

“How do you feel about that?” I ask Óscar .

He looks genuinely confused.

“About Thailand?”

“About parents leaving the egg.”

“It’s safe here.”

“I mean emotionally.”

Óscar considers this.

“You’re asking the wrong generation.”

He is 29. To him, an egg is not a pregnancy removed from a person. It is simply where babies come from. His own parents had him the old way. Full womb.

“Would you do that?”

He laughs.

“My mother had haemorrhoids for six months.”

I write this down.

“Please don’t put that in the article.”

The Woman Who Chose the Womb

Three days later , I am standing outside a prenatal centre in Gràcia.

The sign says:

THE CONTINUITY HOUSE

Underneath:

FULL-GESTATION SUPPORT IN A TECHNOLOGICAL AGE

Inside, everything is beige. The walls are beige. The furniture is beige. The tea is somehow beige. A woman named Elena is sitting on a large cushion with both hands on her stomach.

She is 34 years old and 37 weeks pregnant. Her baby is entirely inside her body. There are no external sensors. No shell. No app. No battery. I find this surprisingly unsettling.

“How do you know everything is okay?” I ask.

Elena looks at me.

“I go to the doctor .”

“Yes, but right now.”

“She moves.”

“And if she doesn’t?”

“I worry.”

“Then what?”

“I continue worrying.”

This is, apparently, the original system. Elena is part of the Full Womb minority. Thirty-one percent of births in Western Europe now happen this way. The number was expected to fall much faster .

Instead, the womb became political. Some people choose it for religious reasons. Some for medical reasons. Some because they distrust technology. Some because they dislike the idea of their unborn child having a charging port nearby.

Elena says she is not ideological. She simply wanted to experience pregnancy. Her friends reacted as if she had announced plans to cross the Atlantic in a wooden boat.

“One asked if I was anti-science.”

“Are you?”

“I have a robot vacuum.”

Her employer was less amused. Elena works for a logistics company. When she announced her pregnancy, her manager congratulated her . Then he asked whether she had considered extraction.

“Immediately?”

“Not immediately. There was a pause.”

“How long?”

“Maybe four seconds.”

“That’s a long pause.”

“It was a very long pause.”

Under Spanish law, employers cannot discriminate based on reproductive model. This law exists because employers discriminate based on reproductive model. Egg parents are considered operationally predictable.

Pouch parents are considered operationally flexible. Womb parents are considered, according to one leaked corporate document from 2052, a “reproductive continuity risk.” The company responsible apologized.

It also changed the phrase. Elena’s pregnancy has been physically difficult. She has back pain. She sleeps badly. She stopped commuting at 34 weeks. I ask whether she ever regrets not choosing an egg.

“Every day.”

I wait.

“Really?”

“No.”

She smiles.

“Ask me at three in the morning.”

The baby moves. I can see it through her shirt. For the first time in my reporting, there is no screen between me and the developing child. No percentage. No green status indicator .

No estimate. Just movement. I ask Elena whether this makes her feel more connected to the baby. She sighs.

“You people always ask that.”

“You people?”

“Journalists. Egg parents. My mother .”

“What should I ask?”

“Ask if I’m comfortable.”

“Are you?”

“No.”

Prenatal Class for Traditionalists

I return to the Continuity House the following morning. Seven pregnant people are sitting in a circle. Their partners sit beside them. The instructor is explaining labour . I had forgotten how violent the diagrams are.

Twenty years of reproductive technology have changed our tolerance for traditional birth. One partner raises his hand.

“Sorry. The whole head?”

The instructor nods.

“Through there?”

“Yes.”

He looks at his pregnant wife. She looks back. Something passes between them. Possibly love. Possibly blame. During the break, I speak with a couple named Dani and Irene. They considered all three models.

They rejected the egg because Dani works in software.

“I know how technology is made,” he says.

They rejected the pouch because Irene did not want an implant. So they chose the womb.

“Do people judge you?” I ask.

“Constantly,” Irene says.

“For being traditional?”

“For being irresponsible.”

This surprises me. She explains. When every reproductive model was biological destiny, pregnancy complications were tragedy. Now they can be interpreted as consumer choice. If Irene develops a problem, someone will ask whether it could have been avoided with extraction.

If her career slows, someone will ask why she chose the least work-compatible option. Choice, she tells me, was supposed to create freedom. Instead, it created new ways to blame people.

Across the room, the instructor calls everyone back. The next topic is pelvic floor recovery. Dani closes his eyes.

A Baby Changes Bodies at 6:15 p.m.

The Pouch Family lives in Sant Cugat. I arrive during the evening transfer . This is not intentional. The train was late.

When the door opens, a man named Joel says:

“Perfect timing. She’s coming out.”

I have known Joel for eleven seconds. His shirt is open. Inside an abdominal pouch is his daughter , Júlia. She is five months old. Or seven months prenatal. The terminology remains controversial.

Her head appears first. She has dark hair and a furious expression. Joel’s wife, Marta, is sitting on the sofa wearing a transfer garment.

“Did she sleep?” Marta asks.

“Forty minutes.”

“Only?”

“Maybe fifty.”

“You said forty.”

“I didn’t time it.”

“You have the app.”

“I was working.”

“You were carrying our daughter .”

“I can do two things.”

Marta looks at me.

“This is why birth rates are falling.”

The transfer is surprisingly calm. Joel sits beside Marta. A nurse showed them how to do this months ago. The baby detaches from Joel’s internal feeding membrane, is supported between their bodies and slowly settles into Marta’s pouch.

It takes four minutes. Júlia complains. Then she disappears. Marta closes the support layer of her shirt.

“That’s it?” I ask.

“That’s it.”

Joel stands. He stretches. Then he walks to the kitchen and opens a beer . The speed of the transformation is remarkable. Four minutes ago, he was biologically sustaining a developing human.

Now he is looking for olives.

“How does it feel when she leaves?” I ask.

“Empty.”

He pauses.

“Physically empty. Don’t make that poetic.”

I write it down.

The Pouch Calendar

The family calendar is displayed on a screen in the kitchen.

Monday:

JOEL — BABY

Tuesday:

MARTA — BABY

Wednesday:

JOEL AM / MARTA PM

Thursday:

GRANDMA 14:00–19:00

Friday:

MARTA — BABY

Saturday has been left blank. I ask why.

“We’re negotiating,” Marta says.

The Spanish Shared Carry Program rewards families that divide biological carrying time. Parents do not have to split it equally. But the closer they get to 50/50, the larger their annual care credit.

The government describes this as encouraging reproductive equality. Joel describes it as “the state turning my abdomen into a spreadsheet.” Last year , a scandal erupted when several wealthy families were found to be paying registered caregivers to carry their babies and collect the equality bonus.

The newspapers called it Pouchgate. Three ministers resigned. One of them had never carried his own children despite appearing in a government campaign with the

slogan:

EVERY BODY CAN CARE.

Marta and Joel are currently at 54/46. Both believe they are the 54. The official app knows the answer . Neither will open it while I am there. At dinner , Júlia moves inside Marta.

The movement is visible. Joel watches.

“See?”

“What?”

“She’s active now.”

“She was active with me.”

“Not like this.”

“She literally kicked me during a meeting.”

“Because she wanted to leave.”

This is the most equal reproductive system ever invented. It has not ended marital conflict. It has generated better data.

Pouch Night

I stay until 2 a.m. At 11:40, Júlia wakes. Marta walks around the apartment. At 12:15, she wakes again. At 1:03, again. The baby is not technically born. She is already ruining everyone’s sleep.

At 1:20, Joel offers to take her . Marta says no. At 1:25, she says yes. They perform the transfer in near darkness. No argument. No calendar . No government incentive. Marta falls asleep almost immediately.

Joel sits on the sofa with Júlia inside him. I sit opposite. For a while, neither of us speaks.

Then he says:

“This is the part people don’t understand.”

“What part?”

He looks down.

“That it’s heavy.”

“The baby?”

“No.”

He searches for the word.

“Being necessary.”

The apartment is silent. I think this may be the most profound thing anyone has said to me during three weeks of reporting. Then his pouch monitor vibrates. He looks at it.

“Sorry. She pooped.”

The Pouch Office

The next morning, I visit Joel’s workplace. His company has 400 employees and nine transfer rooms. The rooms were originally called Biological Care Exchange Facilities. Nobody used them.

Now they are called Baby Rooms. One contains a sofa, a sink, medical equipment and a framed poster:

SHARING THE CARRY IS SHARING THE CARE

Someone has written underneath:

UNLESS MARTA HAS A DEADLINE HR has tried to remove it twice. It keeps returning. Joel tells me pouch parents are generally treated well at the company. Then we enter a meeting. There are six people.

Three are carrying. Nobody mentions it. Twenty minutes into the meeting, one employee’s pouch alarm sounds. He silences it. Five minutes later , it sounds again.

“Do you need to go?” his manager asks.

“No.”

The alarm sounds a third time. The manager looks at him.

“Go.”

He leaves. Nobody reacts. I try to imagine this scene in an office in 2025. Then I remember that in 2025 people attended video calls while actively giving birth. Perhaps humanity has always been strange.

The difference is that now we have better chairs.

Lunch With the Egg Lobby

To understand the politics of 2055, I meet a representative of the European External Development Association. The EEDA represents incubator manufacturers, shell-monitoring companies, egg insurers and something called “prenatal mobility providers.”

We meet at a restaurant that has egg stands beside every table. My lunch companion is named Victor . He dislikes the term Egg Lobby.

“We represent reproductive innovation.”

“Isn’t that what a lobby would say?”

He smiles. Victor believes external incubation is the most important liberation technology since contraception. He has statistics. Egg parents return to work sooner . They report fewer pregnancy-related health complications.

Second parents perform more prenatal care. Single-parent birth rates have increased. So have births among people who previously avoided pregnancy for medical reasons. I ask about subscriptions.

He sighs.

“Here we go.”

“Should the health of an unborn child be behind a paywall?”

“It isn’t.”

“What is behind the paywall?”

“Advanced interpretation.”

“So the data is free?”

“The basic data.”

“And if the basic data shows a problem?”

“The parent is notified.”

“What does the notification say?”

“That advanced interpretation is available.”

Victor puts down his fork.

“You’re simplifying a complex ecosystem.”

I tell him this is what journalists do. He tells me this is why nobody trusts us. Dessert arrives. At the table beside us, an incubator begins alarming. Every person in the restaurant turns.

The parent checks the screen.

“Battery.”

The entire restaurant exhales. Victor points at the scene.

“You see?”

“What?”

“Community.”

The Public Hatchery

My final visit is to a public hatchery outside Barcelona. The building looks like an airport designed by people afraid of corners. Families arrive carrying bags, flowers and portable shell cases.

Some have been waiting seven months. Some have been waiting years. In Room 12, I meet Sofia. She is 42. Her son’s egg has begun to crack. A small line runs across the shell. I ask whether she is excited.

“No.”

“Scared?”

“No.”

“What are you feeling?”

“I need to pee.”

She does not go. The crack grows. A nurse enters. Then leaves. Then enters again. Sofia’s sister arrives with coffee. Nobody drinks it. For forty minutes, almost nothing happens. Then everything does.

The shell moves. There is a sound. A hand appears. A tiny wet hand. Nobody speaks. I have spent three weeks hearing about reproductive models. The economic model. The social model. The legal model.

The biological model. Now a baby is trying to get out of an egg. All theory disappears. The nurse moves closer . Sofia is crying. Her sister is crying. I realize I am crying. This is professionally embarrassing.

The baby emerges. He is furious. He screams. Sofia laughs.

“He sounds like my father .”

The nurse wraps him and places him against her chest. For the first time, Sofia touches the child she has been monitoring for seven months. No screen. No shell. No percentage. Just skin.

Later , I ask whether she would choose the Egg Model again. She looks at the incubator . Then at her son.

“Ask me after I sleep.”

It is the same answer Elena gave me. Different biology. Same parents.

Did Any of This Fix the Birth Rate?

In 2035, governments believed reproduction had a technology problem. People were having fewer children. So governments funded better biology. External incubation. Transferable pouches.

Safer traditional pregnancy. The results initially looked extraordinary. Egg births increased rapidly. Pouch births followed. Full Womb births stabilized. Politicians declared victory.

Then the numbers flattened again. It turned out that people were not avoiding children solely because pregnancy was difficult. They were also worried about housing. Money. Work. Childcare.

Relationships. Climate. Time. The vague feeling that adulthood had become an expensive subscription service nobody remembered signing up for . Technology changed the pregnancy. It did not change the rent.

The most successful birth-rate program in Europe eventually turned out to be embarrassingly boring. Affordable housing. Predictable childcare. Shorter working hours. Protected parental leave.

The ability to imagine the next ten years without feeling financially nauseous. The machines helped. The pouches helped. Medicine helped. But no reproductive technology could solve a society that made people afraid of the life waiting after the baby arrived.

Three Homes

On my last day, I return to the three families. Alma, the egg in the kitchen, is back at 99.6%. Marc has upgraded to Premium. Laura is furious.

“How much?”

“Three months free.”

“That is not what I asked.”

The incubator glows softly. In Sant Cugat, Joel is carrying Júlia. He claims the official numbers now show 51/49. Marta says he is counting a nap incorrectly. They are considering a second child.

“Pouch again?” I ask.

They look at each other .

“Egg,” they say simultaneously.

At the Continuity House, Elena has given birth. Full Womb. Thirty-nine weeks. Fourteen hours of labour . No extraction. No incubator . No pouch. I visit her in the hospital. She looks destroyed.

The baby is sleeping on her chest.

“Still happy with your choice?” I ask.

She stares at me.

“Get out.”

I do. Outside, Barcelona is full of families. A woman pushes an egg stroller across a pedestrian crossing. A man stands at a bus stop with a baby inside his pouch. A heavily pregnant person walks slowly past a pharmacy.

Three reproductive technologies. Three economies. Three political identities. Three completely different ways to bring a human into the world. All of them designed to solve the ancient problem of having children.

None of them solving the ancient problem of being a parent. At 7:42 that evening, my phone vibrates. It is a message from Marc. A screenshot. Alma: 99.8%.

Underneath, he has written:

Do you think this is too high?
